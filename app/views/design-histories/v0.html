Initial assumptions from discovery

The starting context, assumptions, and materials that informed our initial prototype before any user testing.

Date: November 2025 | Phase: Alpha Sprint 1

DHSC conducted a comprehensive discovery phase in March 2025, researching directly with NHS users and producing personas, journey maps and system maps.

The recommendations from the discovery were:

  1. Develop a product value information service to address unmet information needs
  2. Improve sharing of data between trusts, currently via informal information sharing networks
  3. Improve standards of procurement commercial literacy and ensure teams have correct skills
  4. Create standards for evidence provided by suppliers, and improve data collection from trusts

This Alpha phase was kicked off to address recommendation 1. We pivoted to include 2 as well. 3 and 4 have been spun up into separate teams.

Information:

The working analogy: "A Which? Best Buyers Guide for medtech devices, or Trust Pilot for procurement teams"

Problem statement

The NHS currently lacks a consistent, centralised way to compare medical technology products. While valuable evidence exists, it is often fragmented, inconsistent, or difficult to use â€“ resulting in inefficiencies, repeated work, and missed opportunities to adopt high-value technologies.

Four core problems identified

Lack of passporting
Suppliers repeatedly submit the same information to different NHS organisations. No mechanism exists to submit once and have data visible everywhere.
Fragmented data
Evidence about products is scattered across NICE, ODEP, DTAC, individual trusts, and suppliers. No single view exists to compare products holistically.
Outdated practices
Procurement decisions often default to price rather than clinical value. No framework supports value-based comparison at scale.
Slow innovation adoption
High-value technologies face slow adoption because procurement teams lack confidence in evidence. Individual trusts don't benefit from collective intelligence.

Landscape analysis

We mapped existing NHS procurement platforms to understand where Compass would fit. The key finding was that existing platforms optimise procurement processes (tendering, contracts, ordering) but none provide product intelligence (what exists, comparative value, evidence).

Existing platform analysis
Platform Function Gap for Compass
Atamis Tender management and contracts No product comparison or evidence repository
NHS Supply Chain Product catalogue and ordering Limited clinical evidence; price-focused
Find a Tender Contract notices and awards No product-level data
CCS Frameworks Pre-approved supplier lists Approval doesn't equal evidence of value

Initial assumptions (pre-research)

These were the assumptions that shaped our first prototype, ranked by risk:

  1. Suppliers will submit data in standardised formats. The passporting model depends on this. If suppliers won't engage, the entire concept fails.
  2. Users want to compare based on clinical value rather than price. The VBP approach assumes procurement teams will prioritise outcomes over cost.
  3. Quality evidence exists and is accessible. We assumed NICE, ODEP, and trust evaluations would provide meaningful comparison data.
  4. Platform will change procurement behaviour. Having the tool doesn't guarantee adoption or behaviour change.

Value-based procurement framework

The initial prototype used four VBP domains for product comparison:

Clinical Outcomes
Impact on patient health outcomes, clinical effectiveness, evidence base
Patient Safety
Safety profile, adverse event data, risk management
System Integration
Compatibility with existing NHS systems, interoperability, workflow impact
Sustainability
Environmental impact, product lifecycle, long-term value

Initial prototype

The first prototype (built sprint 1) demonstrated how we would test some our initial assumptions:

1. Product comparison interface

  • Search and browse registered products
  • Product cards showing regulatory status (NICE, ODEP, DTAC)
  • Select up to 3 products for side-by-side comparison
  • Links to PIM for technical specifications
  • Trust evaluation summaries

2. Trust evaluation sharing flow

  • Multi-step form for product registration
  • Regulatory evidence sections (NICE, ODEP, DTAC)
  • VBP domain evidence upload
  • Trust evaluation submission
  • Draft save functionality

Initial design decisions

Design decisions and rationale
Decision Rationale
No price data displayed Forces focus on clinical value; price negotiated separately in tenders
Maximum 3 products compared Keeps interface manageable; reflects typical shortlisting behaviour
PIM integration via links Avoids duplicating technical specs; positions Compass as evidence layer
NHS Design System usage Establishes credibility with NHS professionals

What we didn't know yet

  • How do procurement teams actually build confidence in product decisions?
  • What role do peer networks play in current procurement?
  • Will trusts share their evaluations with other trusts?
  • Is standardised VBP scoring meaningful to users?
  • What information do users actually need vs. what we assumed?
  • How do clinicians' needs differ from procurement professionals?
