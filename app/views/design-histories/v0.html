Design History: Ground Zero

The starting context, assumptions, and materials that informed our initial Compass prototype pages – before any user testing.

Date: November 2025 | Phase: Alpha Week 1

Situation

The platform was conceived as a central digital repository to enable NHS trusts to make faster, more consistent, and evidence-based procurement decisions for medical technologies.

Information:

The working analogy: "A Which? Best Buyers Guide for medtech devices" - empowering procurement professionals and clinicians with information to buy products based on holistic value, not just price.

Problem statement

The NHS currently lacks a consistent, centralised way to compare medical technology products. While valuable evidence exists, it is often fragmented, inconsistent, or difficult to use – resulting in inefficiencies, repeated work, and missed opportunities to adopt high-value technologies.

Four core problems identified

Lack of passporting
Suppliers repeatedly submit the same information to different NHS organisations. No mechanism exists to submit once and have data visible everywhere.
Fragmented data
Evidence about products is scattered across NICE, ODEP, DTAC, individual trusts, and suppliers. No single view exists to compare products holistically.
Outdated practices
Procurement decisions often default to price rather than clinical value. No framework supports value-based comparison at scale.
Slow innovation adoption
High-value technologies face slow adoption because procurement teams lack confidence in evidence. Individual trusts don't benefit from collective intelligence.

Scope for the intial alpha:

Less risky assumptions

  • Approved body evaluations (e.g. NICE Medtech Guidance)
  • External evaluations/accreditations (e.g. ODEP)
  • Passporting functionality (e.g. DTAC)
  • Trust-level evaluations of medtech
  • Link to PIM (Product Information Management system)
  • All medtech registered with MHRA – community and secondary care
  • Side-by-side comparison across a range of metrics
  • Data presented in a user-friendly and intuitive UI
  • Data aligned with VBP (Value-Based Procurement) domains

Riskier assumptions

  • Trust reviews, opinions, or ratings
  • Supplier-submitted information
  • Volume data showing usage across NHS
  • Markers for SMEs and UK-manufactured products

Possibly out of scope for now

  • Price data (explicitly excluded)
  • Non-evaluative data belonging in PIM
  • PAQ forms (centralised in PIM)
  • Public access - only key users
  • Evidence validation (Compass centralises, does not validate)

Landscape analysis

We mapped existing NHS procurement platforms to understand where Compass would fit. The key finding was that existing platforms optimise procurement processes (tendering, contracts, ordering) but none provide product intelligence (what exists, comparative value, evidence).

Existing platform analysis
Platform Function Gap for Compass
Atamis Tender management and contracts No product comparison or evidence repository
NHS Supply Chain Product catalogue and ordering Limited clinical evidence; price-focused
Find a Tender Contract notices and awards No product-level data
CCS Frameworks Pre-approved supplier lists Approval doesn't equal evidence of value
Information:

Strategic position: Compass would be the missing "product intelligence layer" – complementary to existing platforms, not competing with them.

Initial assumptions (pre-research)

These were the assumptions that shaped our first prototype, ranked by risk:

  1. Suppliers will submit data in standardised formats. The passporting model depends on this. If suppliers won't engage, the entire concept fails.
  2. Users want to compare based on clinical value rather than price. The VBP approach assumes procurement teams will prioritise outcomes over cost.
  3. Quality evidence exists and is accessible. We assumed NICE, ODEP, and trust evaluations would provide meaningful comparison data.
  4. Platform will change procurement behaviour. Having the tool doesn't guarantee adoption or behaviour change.

Value-based procurement framework

The initial prototype used four VBP domains for product comparison:

Clinical Outcomes
Impact on patient health outcomes, clinical effectiveness, evidence base
Patient Safety
Safety profile, adverse event data, risk management
System Integration
Compatibility with existing NHS systems, interoperability, workflow impact
Sustainability
Environmental impact, product lifecycle, long-term value

Initial prototype features

The first prototype (built Week 1) demonstrated Must Have scope with two core interfaces:

1. Product comparison interface

  • Search and browse MHRA-registered products
  • Product cards showing regulatory status (NICE, ODEP, DTAC)
  • Select up to 3 products for side-by-side comparison
  • Comparison grid showing VBP domain scores
  • Links to PIM for technical specifications
  • Trust evaluation summaries

2. Supplier submission portal

  • Multi-step form for product registration
  • MHRA registration capture (required)
  • Regulatory evidence sections (NICE, ODEP, DTAC)
  • VBP domain evidence upload
  • Trust evaluation submission
  • Draft save functionality

Initial design decisions

Design decisions and rationale
Decision Rationale
No price data displayed Forces focus on clinical value; price negotiated separately in tenders
VBP scores 0-10 scale Simple comparison metric; assumption that evidence can be meaningfully scored
Maximum 3 products compared Keeps interface manageable; reflects typical shortlisting behaviour
PIM integration via links Avoids duplicating technical specs; positions Compass as evidence layer
NHS Design System styling Establishes credibility; consistency with other NHS tools

Proposed success metrics

At ground zero, we defined these metrics to validate our assumptions:

  • 5+ suppliers submit standardised data for one device category
  • 8/10 procurement professionals find comparison tool more useful than current methods
  • Users can find and understand evidence in under 5 minutes
  • Prototype reduces perceived time-to-decision by 20%+ in testing

What we didn't know yet

These questions remained open at ground zero, to be answered through user research:

  • How do procurement teams actually build confidence in product decisions?
  • What role do peer networks play in current procurement?
  • Will trusts share their evaluations with other trusts?
  • Is standardised VBP scoring meaningful to users?
  • What information do users actually need vs. what we assumed?
  • How do clinicians' needs differ from procurement professionals?

Important: Key unknown

We had not yet validated whether users wanted to read documents or talk to peers. This would prove to be a critical finding that reshaped the entire service direction.

Next steps from ground zero

  1. User research: Interview procurement teams and clinicians to validate assumptions
  2. Supplier engagement: Test submission portal with 3-5 medtech suppliers
  3. Prototype testing: Usability sessions with comparison interface
  4. Assumption validation: Prioritise testing riskiest assumptions first
  5. Iteration: Update prototype based on findings before Week 4
Information:

Document purpose: This design history captures the starting context for Compass alpha. It serves as a baseline to compare against later iterations, demonstrating how user research and testing changed our understanding and approach.
